Related: [../principles/evaluation.md](../principles/evaluation.md) §3 (off-policy evaluation — recsys is its home turf), [../principles/monitoring-and-drift.md](../principles/monitoring-and-drift.md) §4 (feedback loops), [serving-patterns.md](serving-patterns.md) §3 (the hybrid shape).

---

Recsys is where every hard problem in this KB shows up simultaneously: the model's decisions generate its own training data (feedback loops), offline metrics correlate weakly with online outcomes (off-policy), the serving shape is the multi-component hybrid (version-coherence skew), and "better engagement" can diverge from "better product" (metric–objective). Treat this doc as those principles instantiated, plus the patterns unique to the domain.

## Production patterns

- **The canonical architecture — retrieval → filtering → ranking → re-ranking:** retrieval (candidate generation: two-tower embeddings + ANN index, plus cheap sources — popularity, co-visitation, followed-creators) narrows millions to hundreds; business-rule filtering (availability, policy, already-seen); a heavier ranker scores hundreds; re-ranking applies diversity/freshness/business constraints. Design consequence: **each stage caps the next** — the world's best ranker cannot recommend what retrieval never surfaced, so *retrieval recall@K on held-out positives* is a first-class metric, monitored separately from ranking quality. Most "the recommender got worse" investigations that start at the ranker end at retrieval or filtering.
- **Two-tower + ANN mechanics:** user/item towers trained jointly; item embeddings batch-computed into an ANN index (FAISS/ScaNN/hnswlib or a vector DB); user embedding computed at request time. This is [serving-patterns.md](serving-patterns.md) §3 exactly — with its version-coherence trap as *the* recsys deploy bug: item index built by tower v7, user tower serving v6 → cosine similarities are between incompatible spaces, recommendations turn plausible-but-off, nothing errors. Co-version the towers and index as one registry artifact; deploy atomically; the golden-prediction seam test scores fixture (user, item) pairs through offline model vs. live index and asserts parity.
- **Cold start as designed paths, not edge cases:** new items (content features / metadata tower until interactions accrue, plus an exploration boost), new users (popularity/context-based until history exists). Both paths are *permanent traffic segments* — slice every metric by user/item tenure ([../principles/evaluation.md](../principles/evaluation.md) §1's slicing; the "+2% overall, −15% new users" example was a recommender).
- **Impression logging as a first-class product:** log what was *shown* (with position), not just clicked — plus the model version and (ideally) propensities. Without impressions you can't compute honest CTR, can't debias positions, can't do off-policy eval. Retrofits of impression logging are painful; build it day one.
- **Freshness tiering:** item index nightly, trending/popularity hourly, user short-term interest in-session (recent-clicks features at request time). Match each component's recompute cadence to its decay rate rather than one global cadence — and monitor each component's staleness independently ([../principles/monitoring-and-drift.md](../principles/monitoring-and-drift.md) §2 layer 1).

## Common pitfalls

- **Offline NDCG is barely evidence.** Logged data reflects the *incumbent's* choices; your new model is evaluated on its agreement with clicks the old policy generated ([../principles/evaluation.md](../principles/evaluation.md) §3 — the three-consecutive-failed-A/B-tests story is a recsys story). Mitigations in practical order: keep a small randomized-exploration slice always-on and evaluate on it; log propensities and use IPS/DR estimators for bigger swings; and treat offline eval as *triage for what deserves an A/B test*, never as the ship decision.
- **Position bias eats naive training:** items shown at rank 1 get clicked because rank 1. Training clicks-as-labels without position correction learns "predict my own past UI." Standard treatments: position as a training feature zeroed/fixed at inference, or inverse-propensity weighting by position.
- **Popularity feedback spiral:** recommend popular → popular gets more engagement → training data says popular is engaging → recommend harder ([../principles/monitoring-and-drift.md](../principles/monitoring-and-drift.md) §4 at product speed). Monitor catalog coverage / effective-catalog-size and long-tail share *as guardrail metrics with the same standing as CTR*; the exploration slice is also the antidote's delivery mechanism.
- **Engagement–objective divergence:** CTR-optimal is clickbait-optimal ([../principles/evaluation.md](../principles/evaluation.md) §1's third question). Mature recsys teams optimize composite/long-horizon objectives (completion-weighted engagement, retention proxies validated against holdout-cohort retention) and hold *abuse/regret* signals (fast unfollows, "not interested" clicks, immediate bounces) as tripwires.
- **Duplicate/near-duplicate leakage in offline eval:** the same user-item interaction appearing in both train and test via session re-logging, or evaluating on interactions that *preceded* training-window interactions (time-travel). Recsys eval needs time-ordered splits per user, with the standard leave-last-k-out done *by time*, not random ([../principles/data-leakage.md](../principles/data-leakage.md) §2/§4).
- **Filter-stage regressions masquerading as model regressions:** a policy/inventory filter change silently empties candidate pools for a segment; ranker metrics look fine (it ranks what it's given). Log candidate-pool size per request per stage; alert on distribution shifts — it's the cheapest recsys monitoring you'll ever add and it catches a whole incident class the model metrics can't see.
